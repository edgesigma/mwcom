About

Going zero to one with AI engineering

More than a list of projects, this page has become a map of my journey from novice to expert in the new domain of AI Engineering.

After more than 15 years in tech roles, I became a novice once again back in January of 2025. Driven by curiosity and stubbornness, I've emerged out the other end with an entirely new skill stack.

The work

A story in four parts

Peak of Mt. Stupid

memory.audio

Built to survive a 12-hour shift. Working the floor at Amazon CLE2, I'd generate audio lessons before work and loop them all day to keep my mind alive over the noise.

Leverage · the move

I deployed it. It went from scripts I ran at home to something I could reach from the warehouse floor — and, once I was building in public on X, from an audience of one to ~100 strangers generating their own lessons. One deploy turned a private survival habit into a product.

Unintended consequences · the ripple

Real users pulled it where I never aimed it — a lesson on one specific piece of industrial equipment; someone asking it to teach them about memory.audio itself. Their feedback pulled me deeper: cognitive assessments grounded in memory-science frameworks, then real research into memory span and information theory, then a multi-voice podcast format. Every step made it bigger.

Constraint · the wall

And bigger was the wall. With no business model it was an expensive hobby — OpenAI and GCP bills with nothing coming back — and all that feature-chasing had quietly turned a memory tool into a weaker copy of Google's NotebookLM. The thing I'd optimized away was my only edge: memory, specifically. So the next move isn't a bigger app — it's a focused refactor, memory.audio as a Claude skill, that keeps the specificity and sheds the infrastructure.

Valley of Despair

Fidget Feed

An app blocker that gives back. Instead of just saying "no," it intercepts your distracting apps and swaps the scroll for interactive fidgets — the dopamine hit without the 45-minute hole.

Leverage · the move

I swapped Copilot for Claude Code. The first stretch I built real UI from my Figma mockups with a Copilot agent; switching to Claude Code around March was the inflection point — iteration speed and output jumped hard. Same designer, same app, different tool, and suddenly the build stopped being the bottleneck.

Unintended consequences · the ripple

This was the first thing I ran like a business, not just shipped. A landing page with lead capture, profiles on Facebook, TikTok, and X, UGC experiments, exhaustive market research, real early-access signups. I set out to build an app and backed into building an entire go-to-market machine — distribution became a second craft.

Constraint · the wall

And that's where I learned the constraint had moved. I had the app, the business model, the audience, the leads — but as a solo developer I couldn't coordinate the closed beta Google requires to publish: a dozen real testers, two weeks. The wall wasn't code or product; Claude Code had solved building. The wall was coordinating people — which, it turns out, is exactly the problem worth solving. I've since stood up AppHive to clear that beta, but for now the job search and the hackathon have the clock.

Slope of Enlightenment

RedLine

Know before you go. Draw a line around the areas you want to avoid, and every incoming ride offer gets a clear go / no-go signal. I built it because I was the driver who needed it.

Leverage · the move

I built it for myself, in about two days. By now the build wasn't the hard part — the last two projects had turned "idea to working on my phone" from a quarter into a weekend. And because I was the user — a driver with the exact problem — I needed no market research and no beta cohort. Idea, build, and real-world test all happened in my own car, on real ride offers, in days.

Unintended consequences · the ripple

Being my own user changed the product. Driving with it, the real constraints — eyes on the road, one-glance decisions — forced a dead-simple go/no-go signal and hands-free voice, features I'd never have speced from a mockup. And because the problem was real, other drivers wanted it without me marketing to them. Usefulness did the distribution the machine couldn't.

Constraint · the wall

Here's what the slope taught me: building had stopped being the constraint. I could ship almost anything now — but a solo, free consumer app has a ceiling no velocity can raise. The limit wasn't my skill; it was the structure. Real leverage doesn't come from building one more app alone — it comes from a system that coordinates people and captures value. That's the whole reason for what came next.

Plateau of Sustainability

NPC

An AI-run agency that dispatches real-world work to local street teams and verifies it got done. The one I'm building now — and the one that puts everything the last three projects taught me into a single system.

Leverage · the move

The move was to stop building tools and build the thing that runs them. In NPC the AI agents do the coordinating and the checking — dispatch a task, a person completes it in the physical world, an agent verifies the proof. I stopped being the single output and started directing a network that produces it. That's the leverage RedLine told me I was missing.

Unintended consequences · the ripple

Building it, I found the value wasn't the labor — it was the proof. The layer where AI verifies the work — that a shelf really is out of stock, that the task really happened — turned out to be the product. NPC quietly became less "people who do things" and more a system that produces verified truth about the physical world.

Constraint · the frontier

So the frontier now is trust. For a business to pay for verified truth, the verification has to be right — catch the real problem without crying wolf. Driving that false-positive rate down is the whole game, and it's where the work lives today. The question stopped being "can I build it" and became "can I make it trustworthy enough to bet a business on."
